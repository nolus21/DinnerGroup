Linking maps with actor diagrams
- the new approach to visualizing change

The dynamic relationships between human and non-human actors allow or block a systemic change to happen. Whereas the network diagrams represent the power relations between the actors, maps represent the result of these actor interactions - their spatial outcomes - the resulting territories the actors inhabit. How can we link these two forms of representation and knowledge to foster systemic and spatial change within the Danish pig industry?

Each of the actors takes its own perspective, uses different 'datasets' and forms its own narratives to break down the systemic complexity. The intersecting nodes might reveal the barriers or enablers of change.



Towards Danish landscape reconfiguration
Denmark will most probably fail to reach its ambitious goal of 70% reduction in CO2 emissions by 20501. In this context, Denmark’s largest land user - min 34,1%2, significant GHG emitter - ca. 7,1% GHG.3, and largest contributor to reduced biodiversity3, the Danish Pig Industry represents one of the greatest barriers to Denmark’s Green Transition4. Despite a limited proportion to GDP3, and employment6, as an industry with a powerful political lobby and a strong cultural position, it has been largely successful to date in constructing the perception that change is politically and economically impossible.

Most of the vastly monospecies Danish landscape is covered by winter wheat and spring barley fields used for pig-fodder.

Relevance and Application
The policy goals and implementation of proposed change take the form of quantified goals discussed in disciplinary silos – focused on technological solutions, with little or no focus on the societal, spatial and environmental implications. The culturally deep rooted Danish pig industry is a contested territory. It consists of actors’ conflicting values, opinions and interests. In this context, through the use of digital technologies, this project explores visualisation methods which could effectively make visible the points of collision, obligatory points of passage, pressure points; the spatial, logistical, economic, and societal enablers as well as obstacles to change within the resulting monospecies Danish landscape. This narrative network approach aims to supplement the standard spatial planning tools such as GIS with the power of network analysis and participation.

Visualising complexity: actor-network
interdependencies and production of space.

The complexity of the pig industry, its interrelated actors, and the opacity of its corresponding spatial and environmental impacts makes an understanding of these interdependencies difficult to a non-expert audience, planners, policymakers and politicians. As, the use and transformation of territories are the result of collective material practices, behavior and decisions embedded in space over time, (IPCC, 2019; Diaz, S. et al, 2019), it is the project’s point of departure, that the challenges posed by climate change (UNEP, 2019) and the deterioration of biodiversity (IPBES, 2019) cannot be met sufficiently, unless a combined spatial–actor-network (SPA-ANT) perspective is adopted. Complex patterns of actor-network relationships create mutual action-outcome dependencies, which produce space. For example, changes in policy regarding pig feed in one region, contribute to land use change or deforestation in another (Zu Ermgassen, 2015). Knowledge of the complex relations between both human and non-human actors co-producing the contested territories through collective narratives is essential to understand the spatial, logistical, economic, and societal barriers as well as enablers of change in the context of the crisis and has long been recognized within the interdisciplinary scholarship (Berkes et al, 2003; DeFries, 2017).

Breaking the silos.

The disciplinary boundaries often define the relationships considered; hence, either ecological or non-human perspectives tend to be oversimplified or disregarded (Pelosi, 2010). Additionally, the dominance of abstract, quantitative approaches to this crisis resulting in a limited palette of data visualization formats as well as the use of spatial and actor-network representations, to interpret existing conditions, neglect the importance and dynamism of the social landscape, obstruct the debate, co-thinking, and participatory environmental governance (Hajer, 2012), in which actor relations, influences and scenarios are collectively addressed and evaluated in spatial and territorial terms (e.g. Kunze et al., 2012). To navigate within the complexity, the future citizens, decision-makers and planners need to be equipped with tools to explore and visualize the interrelation between the spatial dynamics of the crisis and the plurivocality of public debate, contested values, non-human perspectives– in other words the narratives inherent in environmental issues. Here , the project aims to address the following questions:
1) When a singular, highly centralized and specialized industry such as the pig industry shapes the spatial, cultural, political, and biological landscape of a large part of the Danish territory, how can SPA-ANT approach and its visualization address the systemic inertia characterizing the sector?

2) Could a collaborative, participatory digital interface for combined actor-network and territorial mapping be a relevant tool to communicate and analyze the spatial, societal, economic, logistical, regulatory, and ideological interdependencies, obstacles as well as enablers for the green transition?

3) How can the developed theory and methodology serve as a platform to support expert, non-expert debate and scenario making, beyond the fragmentation of disciplinary and narrative perspectives, and of human and non-human interests? How can this eventually become a base for effective environmental governance and planning?

Plurivocal, multispecies, enabling

Three features distinguish the SPA-ANT from existing approaches. First, the plurivocality; each actor may tell her version of the story in different ways. This creates the opportunity for a more informed, collaborative, participatory, democratic process based on diverse sources of data- and knowledge. Second, highly important for environmental governance in the Anthropocene, drawing upon Actor-Network Theory (ANT) (Latour, 1999; 2005; Callon, 1989), it focuses upon the heterogeneity of nodes that extend beyond humans to include the non-human actors e.g. pigs, bees etc., acknowledging their agency and formative role in social action (e.g. what a pig eats conveys to alternative farmers a story about soil fertility etc.). Third, it connects actors and influences to space enabling targeted, spatial policy recommendation and action. The following diagram presents the conceptual framing of the project:

Therefore, based on ANT, Controversy Mapping (CM) and post-representational Cartographic Theory, this project aims to discuss appropriate theory and approaches to visualizing complex actor-network relations of the DK’s pig industry and their spatial impacts. Furthermore the project, through the creative use of digital technologies, aimed to develop a participatory online tool for making interrelations between actors co-producing associated territories visible through collective mapping of the current conditions and associated actor narratives. The SPA-ANT approach and the interface are aimed to be tested with stakeholder groups relevant to each case study and attempts to reveal barriers as well as enablers for the sustainable transition within the industry, through social network and cartographic analysis methods. It attempts to analyze and visualize three controversies forming a base for future collaborative scenario making:

Controversy 1 (C1): Controversy: Pig – feed – biodiversity

Controversy 2 (C2): Controversy: Genetic Modif. – Antibiotics – Industrialization – Global Markets,

Controversy 3 (C3): Controversy: Traditions – Culture – Lobbies – Politics

Post-representational cartographic theory, controversy mapping and visual rhetorics.

Deep research into cartographic theory helped to formulate initial theoretical and methodological foundation for this project, develop visual language, and code for the interface. A. Yaneva and T. Venturini (2010) - state-of-the-art scholars in the field of controversy mapping (CM) were consulted in regards to the development of the SPA-ANT approach and the interface. Yaneva’s (2012) application of the CM method to architecture, by the usage of digital tools to navigate the socio-technical debates around spatial objects revealed a great potential to treat them as dynamic, ’possessing an immense capacity of connecting heterogeneous actors’.
Furthermore, the broad range of applications of ANT in the planning field addresses not only analytical views on theory (Boelens, 2007), but also planning methodologies, and policy recommendations (Healey, 2007). Building upon its historical role (CIAM 1933, Abercrombie 1944), mapping is a key methodology in the contemporary spatial planning and design disciplines (Paez, 2019) challenging established understanding of the world through showing, rather than telling about less-visible dynamics (i.a. technological, social, economic, political, ecological) manifested in space (Weizman 2017, Kurgan et al. 2019). The more recent renewal of mapping traditions (e.g. van Susteren 2005; Simpson et al. 2018), invigorated by new technologies (e.g. GIS, ‘big data’) as ‘a means to make the complex accessible’ (Abrahams et al. 2006) engages a shift from ‘representational’ and ‘critical’ mapping towards the ‘post-representational’ (Della Dora, 2009, Kitchin, 2010), which puts its focus on the interactions and co-authorships between actors producing it, context and audience.
Both approaches, open possibilities for new ways of mapping and seeing territories in the Anthropocene, their governance, societal and educational change. However, both spatial representations (SPA-) and actor-network graphs (-ANT), are rarely juxtaposed as mutually enriching visualization forms revealing actor-network/territorial relationships. The complex actor-network graphs allow often for a ‘thick’ reading (Geertz, 1973) of the systems’ capacity to change, are often however predominantly abstract, non-spatial, challenging for the non-expert audience, and limited in narrative making capabilities. On the other hand, more accessible, spatial representations offer a wider range of formats to analyze and communicate, are usually limited in revealing actor relations.

Engaging the public - networked narratives.

The importance of engaging the public through the use of the participatory visual tools (IAPP) and input on viewpoints and environmental issues is widely recognized (i.a. Sheppard, 2001). Projects revealing their usability in boosting understanding of climate change issues, their mitigation options (i.a Schroth et al., 2009), and better decision making (Wong and Chua, 2001) were conducted. However, the literature as well as the practice to date is significantly less abundant on the use of visual tools within mapping of the spatial–actor (SPA-ANT) relations and actual environmental impact assessment (Oliveira, 2020). Literature review and further expert interviews will help to formulate relevant theoretical background for the project. The developed digital tool (O2) (i.a. Three.js framework for online 3d-interfaces) aims to merge the advantages of Public Participation GIS (PPGIS) with Network Analysis Tools (e.g. Gephi). It aims to provide a web interface for spatial/actor-network analysis with public input (Sieber, 2006) allowing for participation by formation of actor-network/spatial narratives by 2d/3d representations of actors, maps, data visualizations, descriptions etc., and exploration of the data over the web (Evans et al. 1999). In the next phases of the project, snowball sampling, in-depth semi-structured interviews with a handful of starting actors (acknowledged through previous research, press, formal positions, Twitter scraping) representative to each controversy (C1, C2, C3) will be interviewed and invited to use the tool to present their own perspectives, e.g. a pig fattening farmer from Ringkøbing-Skjern will form a narrative around C1. pig-feed-biodiversity forming her links between actors (e.g. pig feed-composition-spring barley, fertilizers- gov. subsidies), upload opinions, form maps and hints for data visualizations.

Towards Scenario making.

Based on the network analysis emerging from the accumulated narratives, negotiation and scenario development methodology (Popper, 2008) will be applied: visualization of explorative scenarios toward comparative possible futures 2030 and 2050 based on identification of barriers of change through network (e.g. Costa et al. 2019) and cartographic analysis of the plurivocal, multi-species mappings and narratives (Ingram, 2019). The project aims to lay further theoretical and methodological foundation for the Scenario Atlas for the Green transition (SAGRE) - Mapping and Visualising Green Spatial Restructuring in the Anthropocene, and continue to engage in the debate with international scholars within the fields of spatial and urban planning, cartographic theory and digital methods.
